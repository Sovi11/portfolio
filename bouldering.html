Bouldering

No ropes. No harness. Just you, the wall, and a problem to solve. Bouldering is chess for your body — and the beta doesn't always come from where you expect.

The sport

What bouldering actually is

Bouldering is climbing on short walls — usually 4–5 metres — without ropes or harnesses, with a crash pad below. Each route is called a "problem," and problems are graded by difficulty. The goal is to reach the top using only the holds marked for that problem.

Movement

Full-body problem solving

Every problem is a sequence of moves — footwork, body positioning, tension, timing. Getting it wrong at move seven means starting over. Getting it right feels like cracking a code.

Mental game

The "beta"

Beta is the sequence of moves used to solve a problem. You can try your own beta or learn from others — but sometimes the obvious solution isn't the right one, and you have to see the wall differently to progress.

Physical

Strength meets precision

Raw grip strength gets you started. Technique — silent feet, hip placement, reading your center of gravity — takes you further. The balance between brute force and finesse shifts as grades go up.

The grade

V-scale progression

Problems run from V0 (beginner) to V17 (world-class elite). Each grade jump is a genuine step up in body tension, contact strength, and spatial problem-solving. The progression is addictive.

Why I climb

Presence by necessity

Most activities let your mind wander. Bouldering doesn't. When you're five moves into a hard problem, you're not thinking about markets or code — you're entirely in the wall. That forced presence is part of why it works as a counterweight to desk-heavy work.

The reset

Physical and mental

An hour of climbing drains a different kind of battery than a day of intellectual work. You leave a session physically spent but mentally clear — which is a rare combination to find in a single activity.

The habit

Problems that wait for you

A problem you couldn't finish today will still be there next week. Coming back to something with fresh eyes — after sleep, after thinking about it — and suddenly seeing the solution is a feeling that never gets old.
